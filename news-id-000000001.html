Incident at a Glance

On June 9, 2025, the Singapore-flagged container ship MV Wan Hai 503 suffered a catastrophic internal explosion, leading to a major fire off the coast of Kerala, India. This interactive report breaks down the timeline of the event, the extensive rescue and firefighting response, the hazardous materials involved, and the significant environmental threat posed by this maritime disaster.

The Human Element: Crew Status

The immediate human cost of the disaster was significant. This section details the status of the 22 crew members aboard the MV Wan Hai 503, highlighting the successful rescue of the majority and the ongoing search for those still missing. Interact with the chart to see a breakdown of the crew's status.

18 Rescued

Evacuated by the Indian Navy and Coast Guard. They were transferred to INS Surat and taken to New Mangalore Port. First aid was administered onboard for the injured.

5-6 Injured

Two individuals suffered severe burns (30-40%) and remain in critical condition. Three others with less severe injuries have been discharged from the hospital.

4 Missing

An ongoing search and rescue operation continues for four crew members: two from Taiwan, one from Indonesia, and one from Myanmar.

The Chemical Threat: Hazardous Cargo

The fire's severity is directly linked to the vessel's cargo. The MV Wan Hai 503 was carrying over 1,000 containers, with more than 140 containing dangerous goods. This section explores the types of hazardous materials onboard, many of which pose a direct threat to marine life and human health. Use the filters to explore the specific chemical risks.

Ecological Fallout

The disaster poses a grave, multi-faceted environmental threat beyond a simple oil spill. With up to 50 containers of toxic chemicals lost at sea and 100 tonnes of bunker oil at risk, the incident occurred during a critical fish breeding season, threatening the delicate ecosystem and the livelihoods of coastal communities.

Acute Environmental Threats

  • Chemical Contamination: Carcinogens, pesticides, and other toxins pose long-term risks.
  • Oil Spill Potential: 100 tonnes of bunker oil threaten marine life.
  • Drifting Debris: Lost containers create navigational hazards for other vessels.
  • Ecological Timing: Incident occurred during peak fish breeding season, amplifying the biological impact.

Projected Debris Drift

INCOIS simulations predict that debris and spilled materials will drift towards the Kerala coastline, primarily affecting areas between Kozhikode and Kochi within days of the incident.

A Disturbing Pattern

This is the second major hazardous cargo disaster off Kerala in three weeks, following the sinking of the MSC ELSA 3. Experts warn the Wan Hai 503 incident poses a "more serious threat" due to the quantity and variety of its chemical cargo, compounding the economic and ecological damage to the region.
